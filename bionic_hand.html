Hand Responsive Bionic Hand

Wireless / standalone human hand controlled bionic hand.

Background/Origin

Our hands are central to how we interact with the world. I became fascinated by the idea of translating natural human motion into mechanical action. Motivated by my interests in mechanisms, control systems, and electronics, I set out to design a bionic hand that could sense and mimic the movements of a real human hand in real time.

Engineering the Hand (Mechanics)

Starting from hand sketches, I iterated through multiple CAD designs to develop finger joints that balanced low friction with compact spacing, preventing unwanted lateral motion while preserving smooth rotation. Each joint uses a peg-and-pin configuration that allows free rotation and enables spring-based actuation. I initially experimented with cable-driven systems using lanyard, rope, floss, and fishing line, but friction from multiple pulley turns introduced excessive resistance and poor repeatability. I ultimately transitioned to a spring-based design, where carefully tuned stiffness ratios allowed each finger to move in a more natural, human-like way. In retrospect, this approach better resembles biological hands, in which tendons behave like springs with different stiffnesses and are driven by rotational motion at the joints.

Engineering the Hand (Electronics)

To actuate the hand, I used stepper motors controlled through dedicated stepper motor drivers. Each driver was connected to an I²C multiplexer, enabling coordinated control of multiple motors from a single microcontroller. I integrated absolute magnetic encoders at each joint to measure finger angles with high resolution, and powered the entire system from a portable 5V battery pack for mobility. Encoder data were streamed wirelessly to the motor controller over ESP-NOW, creating a low-latency link between the wearable exoskeleton and the mechanical hand. This closed-loop setup allowed the hand to respond directly to a simple bend of the user’s finger in real time.

  • Microcontroller: ESP32 DevKit
  • AS5600 Absolute Magnetic Encoder
  • I²C multiplexer
  • Stepper Motors

Final Notes / Next Steps

My next step for this project would be to fully integrate the electronics into the mechanical structure, consolidating wiring, mounting, and power distribution into a single cohesive system. While this stage is primarily mechanical packaging in CAD rather than a new technical challenge, I chose to pause here. With functional electronics, reliable sensing, and working actuation already demonstrated, I consider this a successful working prototype — one that is fully operational in principle, even if not yet packaged in a final, polished form.
